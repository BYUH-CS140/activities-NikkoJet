How to Hug a Porcupine:

Easy Ways to Love the Difficult People in Your Life

About the Book:

BS:

"How to Hug a Porcupine" by Debbie Ellis is a self-help book that uses the analogy of a porcupine to illustrate how to deal with difficult people in your life.

The book suggests that by understanding the "porcupine's" (i.e., the difficult person's) fears and motivations, you can approach them with patience, empathy, and kindness, rather than escalating conflict.

The analogy encourages readers to recognize and respect the boundaries of these individuals, and to find ways to connect with their soft spots, ultimately promoting peaceful interactions.

When porcupines in the outdoors are threatened, the muscles at the base of each quill tighten causing the porcupine to look “puffed out” because the quills stand up on their body. This results in protection from predators, making them look big and untouchable!

We don’t want to mess with a porcupine in its protection mode. It can charge backwards, raise its tail, and it has been known to throw itself at its attacker!

People can be like porcupines. They can have sharp edges and be abrasive when they are threatened or feel they are confronted with conflict. Their reaction can be just like a porcupine; they put up their defenses and try to scare off their perceived enemies.

Ellis writes several tips on how to “hug” a “human porcupine.” Some of the tips she includes in her book are:

    Know Your Porcupine’s Warning Signs. Watch for the things that make your human porcupine pull out its quills. It could be loud words, aggression or conflict.

    Keep Your Quills In. When your porcupine gets activated, you also feel like you should stick out your quills. Ellis advises “Don’t! Take a step back and breathe and don’t let your own quills get the best of you. (p.13)

    Respect Your Porcupine’s Boundaries. Because a porcupine is feeling attacked, it will react to feeling threatened. If we can respect that it feels fear, we can also respect the porcupine’s boundaries to keep it from puffing out those quills!

    Consider the Porcupine’s Real Needs and Fears. Human porcupines have been born soft, but they have had experiences which have hurt them, make them harden and more apt to stick their quills out to protect themselves from danger.

    Find that Soft Spot! Even porcupines have soft spot which can be found on their bellies. Human porcupines also have a soft spot that allows us to bring a smile to their faces. We just have to find the subjects that they care about and learn about those spots. Having a conversation about what they care about can change their emotional readiness to be “all quills” on you!
